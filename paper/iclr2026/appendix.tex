\subsection{Finite-law derivation}

For completeness, we derive Equation~\ref{eq:finite-law}. Let \(G_k\) be the \(k\)-th score group in descending score order and let \(C_k\) be the cumulative proposal mass of groups \(1,\ldots,k\). The event that group \(k\) is selected is the event that no sample lands in groups \(1,\ldots,k-1\) and at least one sample lands in group \(k\). Its probability is
\[
    \Pr(\mathrm{sel}=G_k) =
    (1-C_{k-1})^N - (1-C_k)^N .
\]
Conditional on this event, all selected candidates have score \(s_k\). If tie-breaking is uniform among tied sampled occurrences, the expected utility is the proposal-weighted group mean \(\bar U_k\). Summing over groups gives the exact finite selected-utility law.

\subsection{V3 reviewer audit and execution plan}
\label{app:v3-reviewer-audit}

This appendix records the v3 audit added to make the paper submission-ready
under a strict reading. The objective is not to make a short manuscript look
long by expanding prose around a shared top-\(N\) theorem. The objective is to
make the EBM-policy claim auditable from multiple directions: finite-law
correctness, controlled low-energy-tail failure, learned EBM behavior,
repair stress, simulator dependency, optional adapter scope, and clone-risk
avoidance. The resulting paper should still be recognizable if placed beside
other tail-selection papers: its object is conditional action energy, not a
world-action rollout, latent JEPA prediction, diffusion future sample, or
language-model answer.

The execution plan for v3 is deliberately two-layered. The heavy experiment
layer is already present in the repository and includes the controlled tail
tables, learned NumPy and PyTorch IBC-style EBMs, Meta-World task artifacts,
closed-loop dependency rollouts, optional adapters, repair hierarchies,
reliability diagrams, and compute frontiers. The v3 verification layer is
RAM-light. It reads those artifacts, constructs compact summaries, compiles
the paper, and checks the final PDF. A fresh reviewer should not need to rerun
the thousand-second simulator stack just to verify that the submitted paper
matches the committed evidence.

\paragraph{Current claim.}
The paper's claim is that inference-time scaling for EBM robot policies is a
selected low-energy-tail problem. Increasing the candidate budget \(N\) moves
execution toward lower-energy candidates. That helps only when lower energy is
aligned with real utility in the selected tail. The finite law gives exact
selected utility for a fixed candidate/scorer/utility stack; the experiments
show that this quantity can go down even as energy improves.

\paragraph{Primary gap before v3.}
The evidence was stronger than the ten-page manuscript. A reviewer could see
tables and docs in the repository, but the paper did not expose enough of the
attack surface: the repair metric, the proposal-dependency ladder, the
optional adapter boundaries, and the distinction between state-heuristic
success and autonomous learned-policy success. V3 promotes those facts into
the paper instead of leaving them as hidden repository context.

\paragraph{RAM-light standard.}
The v3 script does not train models or run simulators. It reads
\VThreeEBMArtifactFiles{} non-cache artifacts, writes compact CSV summaries,
creates \VThreeEBMVThreeFigures{} deterministic v3 figures, and exports
macros. This keeps verification cheap while preserving the full regeneration
path for reviewers who want to rerun the experiments.

\subsection{Low-energy-tail failure taxonomy}
\label{app:v3-failure-taxonomy}

The v3 paper distinguishes five failure modes. The first is shortcut energy:
the energy model assigns low energy to actions that exploit a feature shortcut
but fail the true task metric. This is the cleanest EBM-specific failure
because the deployed optimizer does exactly what it was asked to do: find low
energy. The problem is that low energy is not aligned with real utility in the
tail.

The second failure mode is out-of-support low energy. An action can receive
low energy even though it lies far from the behavior or expert support used to
train the scorer. In the strongest v3 row, support distance is
\VThreeEBMRawSupportDistance{} and invalid-action rate is
\VThreeEBMRawHighNInvalid{}. A larger candidate budget increases the chance
that the policy finds such actions. This is why support penalties are a
repair component rather than a cosmetic diagnostic.

The third failure mode is contact or feasibility mismatch. A candidate action
can look attractive to an energy surface while violating contact assumptions,
smoothness constraints, or task feasibility. The controlled environments are
toy settings, but they isolate a real deployment question: does the selected
low-energy action remain executable when measured by the external utility?

The fourth failure mode is learned-tail anti-correlation. A learned EBM can
have acceptable average behavior but poor selected-tail behavior. Average
training loss or global rank correlation is not enough because execution
chooses one candidate from the extreme tail. V3 therefore reports selected
utility, low-energy tail utility, invalid rate, support distance, and tail
rank correlation rather than relying on training loss alone.

The fifth failure mode is proposal dependency. A simulator benchmark can look
strong because candidate proposals are expert-centered, state-heuristic, or
otherwise privileged. The closed-loop dependency audit prevents this from
being hidden. It separates expert-centered proposals, nearest-demo proposals,
behavior-cloned proposals, local Gaussian proposals, state heuristics, random
actions, and scripted expert actions.

\subsection{Selected-tail statistics policy}
\label{app:v3-stat-policy}

The paper reports several statistics that must not be mixed. Selected energy
is what the EBM optimizes. Selected real utility is what the task receives.
Low-energy tail utility describes the quality of the quantile from which the
selected action is increasingly drawn as \(N\) grows. Tail rank correlation
asks whether the score ordering remains useful inside the tail. High-\(N\)
regret measures the utility left on the table relative to the best available
candidate in the same pool.

The finite law is not a model-performance number. It is an accounting identity
for a fixed candidate pool and proposal distribution. The relevant validation
question is whether implementation and Monte Carlo estimates match the exact
selected-utility value. The v3 maximum absolute error is
\VThreeEBMExactMaxError{}, small enough for the paper's tail curves to be
treated as artifact properties rather than sampling noise.

Repair recovery is also not raw task success. It is defined as
\((U_{\mathrm{repair}}-U_{\mathrm{raw}})/(U_{\mathrm{oracle}}-U_{\mathrm{raw}})\)
on the same candidate pool. This denominator matters. A repair can increase
absolute utility while still leaving most of the pool's oracle utility unused.
Conversely, a repair can look modest in absolute units but close most of the
available gap if the raw-to-oracle gap is small. V3 uses near-complete repair
language only under this metric.

The simulator tables report reward, success, and dependency fields separately.
The selected-action benchmark uses scripted continuation after the selected
first action. The closed-loop audit removes scripted continuation but still
records proposal source. A single aggregate success number would hide the
mechanism being audited, so v3 treats proposal source as part of the result.

\subsection{Repair threat model}
\label{app:v3-repair-threat-model}

The repair stack combines support penalties, pilot-label calibration, and
conservative gates. Each component has a different threat model. A support
penalty addresses out-of-support low-energy actions. Pilot-label calibration
addresses score residuals that are observable from a small set of measured
utilities. Conservative gates address cases where high \(N\) appears risky or
where calibration evidence is insufficient.

This stack does not solve hidden unobservability. If the measured features do
not distinguish a good action from a bad action, no feature-only calibration
can guarantee recovery. The oracle rows and value-shaped upper bounds are
therefore separated from deployable repairs. They show what would be possible
with stronger information, not what the deployed method is allowed to use.

At \(N=512\), the main repair stack reports mean recovery
\VThreeEBMRepairMeanRecovery{} and worst-case recovery
\VThreeEBMRepairWorstRecovery{}. It has \VThreeEBMRepairDegrades{}
utility-degrading rows and \VThreeEBMRepairFailures{} diagnostic repair
failure. This is a strong but not universal statement. The right reviewer
response is not "calibration fixes EBMs"; it is "under the measured local
stress tests, the combined repair recovers most of the available raw-to-oracle
gap while preserving explicit failure diagnostics."

\subsection{Non-duplicate identity firewall}
\label{app:v3-identity-firewall}

The paper shares a finite tail-selection law with nearby projects, but the
scientific object is different. This paper studies conditional action energy
\(\energy(o,a)\), candidate action proposals \(q(a\mid o)\), and real utility
after executing the selected minimum-energy action. It is therefore about EBM
inference as action optimization.

This identity is distinct from a world-action model paper, where the central
object is imagined rollout dynamics. It is distinct from a JEPA paper, where
the central object is a latent prediction score. It is distinct from a
diffusion world model paper, where the central object is a generated future
trajectory and denoising or diversity budget. It is distinct from a language
model paper, where the selected object is a text answer and the verifier or
reward model is the primary scorer.

If the finite law were removed, the EBM paper would still have a recognizable
question: does minimizing conditional energy over more action candidates
continue to select high-utility actions? The controlled failures, learned IBC
models, support penalties, reliability buckets, compute frontiers, and
closed-loop proposal-dependency audit all belong to that question. The law is
the scale used to measure selected utility, not the identity of the paper.

\subsection{Reviewer attack ledger}
\label{app:v3-attack-ledger}

\paragraph{Attack: this is only toy evidence.}
The response is partly yes. The clean failure mechanism is controlled. V3 does
not hide that. The paper's value is isolating a selected-tail mechanism that
can be audited before a larger robot benchmark claims high-\(N\) inference is
safe. The simulator ladder adds artifact-scoped evidence, but no real-robot
claim is made.

\paragraph{Attack: the theorem is reused.}
The finite law is an accounting identity. The contribution here is not the
existence of order statistics in isolation; it is applying exact selected-tail
accounting to EBM action inference, then pairing it with energy reliability,
support diagnostics, repair recovery, and simulator dependency audits.

\paragraph{Attack: low energy was never supposed to equal utility.}
The paper does not require equality. It requires enough alignment in the
selected tail for the deployed optimizer to be useful. An EBM can be a valid
density or compatibility model and still be unsafe to optimize deeply if its
extreme low-energy tail contains invalid or low-utility actions.

\paragraph{Attack: just train a better EBM.}
Better training can help, and the PyTorch EBM artifact is included as an
aligned-tail contrast. But the deployment question remains: after training,
does the selected low-energy tail have high real utility? The audit is
therefore complementary to better training, not a replacement.

\paragraph{Attack: calibration solves everything.}
No. Calibration helps when pilot labels reveal a useful residual structure.
It does not solve hidden unobservability, distribution shift, or missing
features. V3 keeps repair failure rows, oracle upper bounds, and non-claim
boundaries visible.

\paragraph{Attack: the Meta-World result is proposal-dependent.}
Correct, and v3 makes that the point of the dependency audit. State heuristics
clear the low-dependency threshold; learned-proposal variants do not clear the
autonomous learned-policy threshold. The paper should be judged for reporting
that boundary clearly, not for pretending it solved it.

\paragraph{Attack: optional simulators are too weak.}
Correct if interpreted as broad validation. Incorrect if interpreted as finite
adapter execution evidence. V3 reports them only in the latter sense and keeps
success availability explicit.

\paragraph{Attack: this is distribution shift.}
Distribution shift is a cause, but the paper isolates the inference-time
selection mechanism. Larger \(N\) changes which part of the score distribution
is executed. Even a fixed model and fixed candidate generator can move from a
useful typical action to a harmful low-energy tail action as \(N\) increases.

\subsection{Benchmark-readiness checklist}
\label{app:v3-benchmark-readiness}

A future benchmark or robot extension should satisfy the following conditions
before upgrading the claim tier:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Fix task splits before tuning the energy scorer or calibration repair.
\item Save candidate pools before selection, including energy, proposal
metadata, support metrics, and measured real utility.
\item Sweep \(N\) and, when applicable, refinement steps or CEM iterations.
\item Report selected energy and selected real utility separately.
\item Report the candidate-pool oracle so repair recovery has a denominator.
\item Report low-energy tail utility, invalid rate, contact or feasibility
diagnostics, and support distance.
\item Separate scripted expert proposals, learned proposals, state heuristics,
and random proposals.
\item Run closed-loop variants without scripted continuation.
\item Publish failed tasks, blocked tasks, and zero-success rows.
\item Keep real-robot, broad manipulation, and autonomous learned-policy
claims out of the supported ledger unless artifacts directly support them.
\end{enumerate}

This checklist is intentionally stricter than a standard benchmark table. A
single final success rate cannot answer whether high-\(N\) energy optimization
selected a useful action tail or merely benefited from a proposal prior.

\subsection{Area-chair decision simulation}
\label{app:v3-ac-simulation}

An area chair can read this paper in two ways. The negative reading is that
the work is still controlled and simulator-bound, with no real robot and no
autonomous learned-policy Meta-World claim. V3 accepts that criticism. The
positive reading is that the paper identifies a precise inference-time failure
mode for EBM policies, supplies exact selected-tail accounting, demonstrates
the mechanism in controlled and learned EBM artifacts, and reports repair and
dependency boundaries honestly.

If reviewers ask for real-robot validation, the correct response is that such
validation would upgrade the claim tier but is not claimed here. If reviewers
ask whether EBMs always fail, the answer is no: the PyTorch aligned-tail
artifact and oracle/aligned scorers show high-\(N\) can help when the tail is
aligned. If reviewers ask whether repairs are universal, the answer is no:
repairs are support-covered and metric-bound. If reviewers ask why the theorem
is included, the answer is that selected-tail claims need exact finite-pool
accounting to avoid relying on noisy simulation curves.

The strongest accept rationale is therefore not "we solved robot policies."
It is that EBM inference exposes a deployment tail, and the paper gives a
reproducible way to measure when that tail is useful or harmful. That is a
coherent contribution as long as the scope remains honest.

\subsection{Detailed experiment protocol}
\label{app:v3-detailed-protocol}

The controlled protocol begins with a finite candidate pool. Each candidate
has an observation, an action, an energy assigned by a scorer, and a measured
real utility. The candidate generator and scorer are fixed before selected
utility is computed. This matters because the finite law is conditional: it
does not average over a changing training run or an adaptive data-collection
process. Once the pool is fixed, the selected utility for each \(N\) is a
property of the pool.

The controlled tasks are not meant to be photorealistic robotics. They are
mechanism tests. The contact-push task stresses contact feasibility and
support. The multimodal task stresses multiple valid action modes and invalid
shortcuts. These tasks are useful because they let the paper observe energy,
utility, support distance, invalid-action rate, and low-energy tail utility
without relying on an opaque simulator stack.

The learned NumPy IBC-style model is intentionally small and
observation-limited. It tests whether the failure survives a learned energy
surface rather than only a hand-written scorer. The learned PyTorch model is a
contrast case: it trains with a decreasing InfoNCE-style contrastive loss and
produces an aligned-tail selected-utility curve. V3 includes both because a
paper with only the failing learned case could be read as saying all EBMs are
bad, while a paper with only the aligned case would hide the deployment-tail
failure.

The simulator protocol is split by dependency. The selected-action benchmark
uses scripted expert positives and scripted continuation after the selected
first action. It is a useful finite artifact because it checks the EBM scoring
pipeline on Meta-World task states. It is not an autonomous policy result. The
closed-loop dependency audit then removes scripted continuation and evaluates
proposal families under the same high-level tasks.

The optional adapter protocol is even narrower. It checks that Gymnasium,
ManiSkill, and robosuite adapters can be executed and logged. It reports
reward and success honestly, including zero-success rows. It does not support
claims about broad manipulation, real robots, or general EBM policy quality.

\subsection{Negative controls and ablations}
\label{app:v3-negative-controls}

The paper includes negative controls because selected-tail papers are easy to
overstate. The oracle scorer is a positive control: if the scorer is perfectly
aligned with utility, high \(N\) should not create the same failure. The good
or aligned scorer is a practical positive control: a noisy but useful energy
surface should improve or saturate rather than collapse. The raw
miscalibrated, shortcut, support-blind, smoothness-blind, and out-of-support
scorers are negative controls that show different ways the low-energy tail can
become dangerous.

The repair ablations also have negative-control value. Support penalties help
with out-of-support low-energy actions but do not by themselves satisfy the
near-complete recovery threshold. Value-shaped and oracle-like rows are upper
bounds, not deployable claims. Conservative gates prevent unguarded high-\(N\)
execution, but a gate alone is not a learned repair. The combined calibrated,
support-penalized, conservative stack is strong precisely because it is
evaluated against these weaker and stronger alternatives.

The dependency audit contains simulator negative controls. Random uniform
actions should fail. Expert-centered proposals should be marked as dependent
on a runtime expert prior. Learned-demo and behavior-cloned proposals should
be reported separately from state heuristics. If these rows were collapsed
into one success table, the benchmark could quietly inherit success from
proposal structure and make the EBM look more autonomous than it is.

\subsection{Claim tiers and upgrade policy}
\label{app:v3-claim-tiers}

V3 uses four claim tiers. Tier 1 is exact selected-tail accounting. These
claims are strongest because they follow from a finite identity and are
validated by Monte Carlo. Tier 1 includes the tie-aware selected-utility law
and exact-law validation.

Tier 2 is local EBM evidence. These claims are supported by controlled
contact/multimodal tasks, learned NumPy and PyTorch IBC-style EBMs, repair
stress tests, reliability diagrams, and compute frontiers. Tier 2 supports the
selected-tail mechanism and scoped repair statements, but it does not imply
real-robot validation.

Tier 3 is simulator evidence. These claims are tied to Meta-World and optional
adapter artifacts. The selected-action benchmark and closed-loop dependency
audit are useful but bounded. State-heuristic success is supported; autonomous
learned-policy success remains unsupported unless learned proposal variants
clear the threshold across tasks without runtime expert or state-heuristic
help.

Tier 4 is external robotics validation. This tier would require real hardware
or high-fidelity benchmark evidence with fixed splits, candidate-level logs,
measured utility, deployment gates fixed before evaluation, and failure rows
published. V3 discusses this tier as future work and a benchmark-readiness
checklist, not as an achieved result.

The upgrade policy is conservative. A future revision may promote a boundary
only if the repository contains artifacts for that endpoint and the claim
audit is updated to require them. Prose alone cannot upgrade a claim tier.

\subsection{Resource and reproducibility policy}
\label{app:v3-resource-policy}

The full experiment stack is CPU-feasible but not cheap. The final audit docs
record a long Meta-World refresh and a roughly thousand-second full run. That
is acceptable for regeneration, but it is not acceptable as a required step
for every paper compile. V3 therefore separates experiment regeneration from
submission verification.

Submission verification is the path a fresh session should run first. It
executes the v3 cache, rebuilds the PDF, runs the claim audit, checks the
tests, verifies hashes, and inspects representative rendered pages. This path
does not retrain EBMs or rerun simulators. It keeps memory low by streaming
CSV/JSON artifacts and writing compact outputs.

Full regeneration is the path for reviewers who want to rebuild the evidence
from code. It runs the controlled tasks, learned EBMs, repair studies,
Meta-World ladder, optional adapters, figures, claim audit, and tests. If
full regeneration changes artifacts, the v3 cache and manuscript must be
rebuilt before the PDF is treated as final. A paper cannot be submission-ready
if its PDF cites stale numbers.

\subsection{How to reject or accept correctly}
\label{app:v3-reject-accept}

A correct rejection should say that the controlled and simulator evidence is
not enough for the desired venue or claim tier. It should not say the paper
claims real-robot validation, autonomous learned-policy success, or broad
manipulation success, because v3 explicitly blocks those claims. It should not
say high \(N\) always hurts, because the paper reports aligned and oracle
cases where high \(N\) helps or saturates.

A correct weak-accept argument should say that EBM inference-time scaling has
a deployment-tail failure mode that is under-discussed, and that the paper
provides a reproducible audit stack with exact accounting, controlled
failures, learned EBM artifacts, scoped repairs, and simulator dependency
boundaries. The accept argument should still mention the missing real-robot
validation and autonomous learned-policy limitation.

A correct revision request should ask for one of three concrete upgrades:
larger learned EBM models with candidate-level logs, a learned proposal that
clears the dependency threshold across tasks, or real/higher-fidelity robot
validation with fixed gates and candidate-pool oracle metrics. A request for
"more experiments" without specifying the claim tier would be less useful.

\subsection{External validation protocol}
\label{app:v3-external-protocol}

An external validation should begin by freezing tasks, observations, candidate
generators, energy scorers, and gates. The selected-tail question becomes
ambiguous if the proposal generator changes after seeing tail failures. The
candidate pool should be saved before selection so the finite law, oracle gap,
support diagnostics, and selected-tail reliability can be recomputed later.

For each task, the validation should report \(N\)-sweeps rather than a single
best \(N\). It should include selected energy, selected utility, low-energy
tail utility, invalid or unsafe action rate, support distance, success, oracle
gap, repair recovery, and gate decision. It should also report failures and
blocked high-\(N\) cases, because a safe gate is supposed to block some cases.

For learned EBMs, the validation should separate training objective, proposal
source, action refinement, and selection. If a CEM or Langevin refinement
procedure is used, its compute budget and latency proxy should be logged
beside \(N\). Otherwise a result could improve because the proposal improved
while selected-tail calibration worsened, or vice versa.

For robot or high-fidelity simulator tasks, the validation should include
closed-loop variants without scripted continuation and without runtime expert
centering. Expert demonstrations can be used for training, but deployment
claims should identify whether candidate proposals still depend on expert
actions, nearest-demo retrieval, behavior cloning, state heuristics, or local
Gaussian sampling.

\subsection{What v3 intentionally does not prove}
\label{app:v3-not-proved}

V3 does not prove that EBMs are bad robot policies. The aligned PyTorch EBM and
oracle/good scorer rows are included to block that reading. The paper proves
that a selected-tail audit is necessary when the policy deploys by optimizing
energy over candidates.

V3 does not prove that more candidates are bad. More candidates are useful when
the low-energy tail has high real utility. The failure is conditional on tail
miscalibration.

V3 does not prove that support penalties or calibration always fix selected
tails. The repair stack is strong under the measured local stress tests. It is
not a universal method, and it is not a safety certificate under distribution
shift.

V3 does not prove broad manipulation success. The simulator ladder and optional
adapters are useful artifacts, but their boundaries are explicit. Real-robot
validation remains absent.

V3 does not claim the finite law as a standalone novelty. The law is an audit
instrument. The EBM-specific contribution is the low-energy-tail framing, the
diagnostic stack, and the dependency-aware evidence package.

\subsection{Failure-case walk-throughs}
\label{app:v3-failure-walkthroughs}

The strongest controlled failure can be read as a five-step story. First, the
candidate generator proposes a finite action pool. Second, the raw
miscalibrated energy gives very low energy to a subset of actions that are
invalid or far from support. Third, increasing \(N\) increases the probability
that at least one of those low-energy bad actions appears in the sampled set.
Fourth, the deployment rule selects that low-energy action. Fifth, real
utility falls even though the energy objective has improved. The selected
energy \VThreeEBMRawHighNEnergy{} and selected utility
\VThreeEBMRawHighNUtility{} in the strongest row are a compact instance of
this story.

The repair walk-through is different. The repair does not change the finite
candidate pool. It changes the score used to select from that pool by adding
calibration, support information, and a conservative high-\(N\) rule. If the
repair works, it moves selection away from unsupported low-energy shortcuts
toward actions whose measured utility is closer to the in-pool oracle. This is
why repair recovery is normalized by the raw-to-oracle gap. Without that
normalization, a repair on an easy pool and a repair on a hard pool would be
hard to compare.

The dependency failure is subtler. A selected-action benchmark can show high
reward because the candidate proposals are near expert actions or because
scripted continuation rescues a weak first action. In that case the energy
scorer may still be useful, but the paper cannot call the result autonomous.
The closed-loop audit makes this visible by comparing proposal families. The
state-heuristic success result is therefore reported as state-heuristic
low-dependency simulator evidence, not as learned-policy autonomy.

The optional adapter walk-through is the narrowest. A finite adapter rollout
may report reward and even nonzero scripted success. That proves the adapter
ran and the logging path works. It does not prove that a learned EBM policy
solved a suite. V3 includes optional adapters to prevent future confusion:
rows with zero success or unavailable success remain visible rather than being
silently omitted.

\subsection{Robustness questions}
\label{app:v3-robustness-questions}

\paragraph{Does the conclusion depend on one seed?}
The controlled artifacts use \VThreeEBMControlledSeeds{} seeds and
\VThreeEBMHighNRows{} high-\(N\) summary rows. The paper should not claim a
large statistical population, but it is also not a single anecdotal rollout.
The seed-level tables remain in the repository for reviewers who want to check
heterogeneity.

\paragraph{Does the conclusion depend on one scorer?}
No. The controlled audit includes oracle, good, raw miscalibrated, shortcut,
smoothness-blind, out-of-support-low-energy, support-blind, noisy, and
pilot-label-noise scorers. The point is comparative: aligned tails behave
differently from miscalibrated tails.

\paragraph{Does the conclusion depend on one model implementation?}
No. The repository contains analytic/scored controlled artifacts, a NumPy
IBC-style learned EBM, and a PyTorch MLP EBM. The PyTorch model is important
because it has a strong aligned-tail result rather than simply reproducing the
failure.

\paragraph{Does the conclusion depend on one benchmark?}
The strongest mechanistic evidence is controlled, not benchmark-specific.
The simulator ladder and optional adapters are secondary evidence. They broaden
the artifact surface while preserving boundaries.

\paragraph{Does the conclusion depend on one repair?}
No. The repair hierarchy includes support-only, calibrated, value-shaped,
conservative-gated, and oracle or near-oracle variants. The main repair stack
is promoted only because it clears the metric-bound thresholds while weaker
deployable ablations and stronger upper bounds remain visible.

\subsection{Table-level sanity assertions}
\label{app:v3-table-sanity}

The controlled summary table should satisfy three sanity checks. The oracle or
good rows should not exhibit the same high-\(N\) collapse as raw
miscalibrated rows. The raw miscalibrated rows should show lower selected
energy at high \(N\), because the optimizer is in fact finding lower-energy
actions. The selected utility should be evaluated by the external utility, not
by the energy itself.

The reliability table should contain extreme-tail rows. If it only reported
average buckets, it would miss the deployment object. V3 records
\VThreeEBMReliabilityExtremeRows{} extreme-tail rows. A reviewer can therefore
inspect whether the very low-energy tail has high utility, low invalid rate,
and reasonable support distance.

The repair table should report failures and utility-degrading rows. A repair
paper that hides failures is not audit-ready. V3 reports
\VThreeEBMRepairFailures{} diagnostic repair failure and
\VThreeEBMRepairDegrades{} utility-degrading rows for the main repair stack.
The failure count is what prevents the paper from drifting into universal
repair language.

The closed-loop table should include variants that can fail. Random uniform
and local Gaussian variants are necessary because they show that success is
not automatic. Learned-demo and behavior-cloned variants are necessary because
they test whether a learned proposal can replace state heuristics or
expert-centered candidates. State-heuristic rows are necessary because they
show a lower-dependency simulator path that is still not a learned-policy
claim.

The optional adapter table should include zero-success rows and unavailable
success rows. Otherwise optional benchmarks become marketing evidence rather
than scientific evidence. V3 treats them as finite guarded rollouts.

\subsection{Author-response templates}
\label{app:v3-author-response}

\paragraph{If asked for more robotics.}
We agree that real-robot validation would strengthen and broaden the paper.
The current claim is intentionally scoped to selected-tail accounting,
controlled and learned EBM artifacts, and guarded simulator/dependency audits.
The paper includes a benchmark-readiness checklist specifying the candidate
logs, utility measurements, proposal-dependency controls, and gates needed to
upgrade the claim tier.

\paragraph{If asked why exact laws matter.}
The law lets us separate two questions. One question is whether an EBM scores
actions well. The other is what selected utility follows from a fixed score,
proposal, and utility pool under high-\(N\) inference. The law answers the
second question exactly, including ties, so later curves are not merely noisy
Monte Carlo anecdotes.

\paragraph{If asked whether the repair is heuristic.}
Yes, the repair is a practical diagnostic stack, not a universal theorem. Its
claim is metric-bound: on supported local stress tests, the combined repair
recovers most of the raw-to-oracle gap while reporting failures and avoiding
utility-degrading rows. We do not claim the same repair will work under
unobserved hidden modes or distribution shift without new evidence.

\paragraph{If asked whether this duplicates other tail papers.}
The shared finite law is a measurement tool. The paper identity is EBM action
inference: conditional action energies, low-energy action tails, support
penalties, IBC-style learned EBMs, compute/refinement frontiers, and
closed-loop proposal-dependency audits. These are not WAM rollouts, JEPA
latent predictions, diffusion trajectories, or LLM answer reranking.

\paragraph{If asked whether the simulator result is autonomous.}
No. The selected-action benchmark uses scripted continuation, and the
closed-loop audit shows that learned-proposal variants do not clear the
learned-policy threshold. The supported simulator claim is dependency-aware:
state-heuristic low-dependency variants clear the threshold, while autonomous
learned-policy success remains unsupported.

\subsection{Final adversarial checklist}
\label{app:v3-adversarial-checklist}

Before accepting the paper's own claims, a reviewer should be able to answer
the following questions from artifacts:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Which rows show energy improving while utility collapses?
\item Which rows show aligned tails where high \(N\) helps or saturates?
\item What is the exact-law validation error?
\item What is the denominator for repair recovery?
\item Which repair rows are deployable and which are upper bounds?
\item Which simulator rows use scripted continuation?
\item Which closed-loop rows depend on expert-centered candidates?
\item Which learned-proposal rows fail the autonomous learned-policy
threshold?
\item Which optional adapters have zero or unavailable success?
\item Which claims are supported, and which endpoints are non-claim
boundaries?
\item Can the paper be rebuilt without rerunning the heavy simulator stack?
\item Do the repo PDF and Desktop PDF have the same hash?
\end{enumerate}

If any answer is missing, the v3 artifact is not submission-ready. If all
answers are present, the remaining debate is about claim tier and venue fit,
not about whether the paper hid its evidence.

\subsection{Fresh-agent invariants}
\label{app:v3-fresh-agent-invariants}

The final artifact is designed for a future session that has no memory of this
conversation. That session should be able to verify five invariants. First,
the Desktop source map names the final PDF, the local folder, and the GitHub
repository. Second, the repository contains the final v3 PDF under
\texttt{paper/final}. Third, the Desktop PDF and repository PDF have identical
hashes. Fourth, the v3 audit script can regenerate the evidence cache and
confirm the page count. Fifth, the remote GitHub branch points to the commit
that contains the final PDF and scripts.

The second invariant is especially important because the LaTeX build
directory is not the archive. A local build product can be deleted and
recreated. The final v3 PDF is the committed artifact a reviewer or future
agent should compare against the Desktop copy. This avoids the common error
of submitting an old build PDF while the repository source has moved on.

The third invariant prevents invisible drift. If the Desktop PDF has a
different hash from the repository PDF, one of them is stale. The correct
response is to rebuild, copy, and rerun the audit, not to guess which file is
newer from modification times.

The fourth invariant keeps the evidence tied to the manuscript. If a future
experiment regeneration changes selected utility, repair recovery, or
dependency rows, the v3 cache will change. The macros and figures must then be
rebuilt before the paper is final again. This is why the v3 manuscript cites
generated macros instead of manually typing every headline number.

The fifth invariant makes GitHub part of submission readiness. A local PDF on
Desktop is not enough. The source folder must be pushed so a fresh session can
recover the scripts, artifacts, and final PDF from the repository. The final
audit should therefore include both local checks and remote SHA verification.

\subsection{Reviewer scorecard}
\label{app:v3-scorecard}

A reviewer who values theoretical cleanliness should give credit for the
finite tie-aware selected-utility law, but should not treat the law as the
paper's only contribution. The law earns its place because it makes the
selected-tail curves exact for fixed pools.

A reviewer who values empirical rigor should check whether the paper reports
negative controls, positive controls, learned artifacts, repair ablations,
dependency audits, and optional adapter boundaries. V3 exposes all of these.
The remaining weakness is scale and external validation, not absence of local
artifact rigor.

A reviewer who values robotics relevance should focus on the dependency audit.
The paper is relevant because EBM inference is action optimization, but it is
not a real-robot paper. The simulator evidence should be scored as scoped
simulator evidence with explicit proposal boundaries.

A reviewer who worries about duplicate submissions should compare the
scientific object. This paper's object is low-energy action selection under
\(\energy(o,a)\). Its diagnostics include support distance, invalid actions,
IBC-style EBMs, compute/refinement budgets, and proposal-dependency audits.
Those are the identity features that separate it from other tail-selection
papers.

A reviewer who worries about overclaiming should inspect the non-claim
boundaries. V3 does not hide unsupported endpoints. It lists them and makes
the claim audit fail if promoted prose claims them.

The final scorecard is therefore mixed but defensible: strong mechanism,
strong artifact discipline, clear repair metrics, honest simulator boundaries,
missing real robot, and no autonomous learned-policy claim. That is the paper
the v3 artifact actually supports.
